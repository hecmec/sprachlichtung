## Summary

The second session on **fallacies of presumption** covers the **oversimplification / reduction** subgroup, making a complex issue look simpler than it is by focusing on a limited aspect of it. Five fallacies are presented.

### 1. False dichotomy (faulty dilemma / bifurcation / excluded middle)

Oversimplifying by reducing the available options, usually presenting **two choices as the only choices**. Structurally it mimics a valid disjunctive syllogism ("either P or Q; not P; therefore Q"), but it is fallacious when the either/or premise is **false** because **other options exist**. The number of options need not be two, only fewer than the real possibilities. Examples: "either evolution is true or creation is true"; "you said you'd never vote for Trump, so you must be voting for Biden" (ignoring third parties or not voting).

### 2. Nothing-but (reductive) fallacy

Treating **one aspect of a thing as the whole** of it, "X is nothing but Y", when the thing is actually more complex. Examples: "man is nothing but an animal" (true that he is an animal, but it ignores what distinguishes humans); "thoughts are nothing but electric impulses in the brain" (behaviorism); "taxation is theft."

### 3. Category mistake

Confusing **kinds, types, or natures**, imposing the principles of one category on something in a different one (an apples-to-oranges error). It often confuses **disciplines and their methods** (using math to settle history) or **roles and relationships** (prescriptions vs. descriptions). Examples: "prove to me mathematically that there was a Civil War"; "what does red smell like?"; "what happened in the moments *before* time began?" (there is no "before" without time).

### 4. Argument from the beard (continuum / sorites / line-drawing / bald-man fallacy)

A fallacy about matters of **degree**: arguing that because you **cannot identify the precise point** where one extreme becomes another on a continuum, there is **no difference** between the extremes (when does stubble become a beard?). The assumption, that summing small differences never yields a big one, is false: one is clearly not one million. The **larger the spread, the more fallacious** the argument. Examples: "everyone is more or less good and bad, so don't call Stalin evil and Mother Teresa good"; "a single hair won't make me bald, so I'll never go bald."

### 5. Fallacy of moderation (golden mean)

Assuming the answer **always** lies in the middle between two extremes, the opposite error to the false dilemma. The correct conclusion depends on the specific premises: sometimes moderation is right, sometimes an extreme is. Unlike Aristotle's virtue (a mean between vices), **truth is not a compromise**. It appeals psychologically to those who want to avoid conflict and appease both sides. Example: pushing a reluctant skydiver out at 2,000 feet as a "compromise", worse than either original option.
