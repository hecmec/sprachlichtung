## Summary

Continuing the theme of **persuasion vs. proof**, this lecture examines how businesses use **psychological** rather than logical means to sell. Advertising leans heavily on **images**, which are neither true nor false (only claims have truth value) but are powerfully persuasive and easily doctored. Ads typically carry **little or no real product information**; the goal is to move you emotionally.

### Market research and marketing errors

Business exists to make a profit, so research aims to identify the **target market** and its "hot buttons" using **inductive methods**: surveys, focus groups, observation, sales/ratings data (e.g. Nielsen), and experimentation. Common errors illustrate cognitive biases:

- **Confirmation bias** – only seeking supporting evidence (Datsun/Nissan ignoring that their small engines were wrong for U.S. roads).
- **Escalation of commitment / loss aversion** – sticking with a failing path (Sony pushing Betamax against the cheaper VHS for years).
- **Ignoring culture** – the Gerber baby logo backfiring where labels show the contents; Datsun's "Fair Lady" renamed the 240Z for U.S. buyers.

Strategy is often framed with **SWOT** (Strengths, Weaknesses, internal; Opportunities, Threats, external), as seen in Walmart's pricing, Nestlé reacting to Starbucks, and Lego's pivot into video games and films. **Brand recognition** (Apple, McDonald's, Coca-Cola logos) works through simple, memorable design plus constant repeated exposure.

### How ads reach us

The three purposes of media advertising are to create **awareness**, **inform**, and above all **motivate demand and brand loyalty**. We meet ads everywhere (hundreds per day), including **product placement** embedded in shows, films, and sports, matched by demographic research to the audience. **Children under ~8** cannot recognize advertising intent or its fallacies, which is why they are especially vulnerable.

### Common advertising techniques

- **Scarcity** – "limited time only", limited editions; cutting off your time to compare alternatives (the disciplined response is to do your own research).
- **Association** – linking a product to a desirable image: **transference** (celebrity glamour), **class/snob appeal** (selling a lifestyle), **sensuality** ("sex sells"), and **wish fulfillment** (get-rich-quick fantasies).
- **Authority** – celebrity endorsements as an **appeal to a false authority** (*argumentum ad verecundiam*); the celebrity rarely has real expertise about the product.
- **Commitment** – small initial buy-in leading to more (network marketing); wearing a cause's ribbon making donation more likely.
- **Personality appeal** – we buy from people we like (Tupperware/Pampered Chef parties among friends).
- **Consensus / bandwagon** – "best-selling", "billions served" (*consensus gentium*, appeal to the majority).

### Manipulating language and claims

A recurring lesson: ads are crafted so that what *sounds* like a claim often is not.

- **Vague claims** kept safe from false-advertising liability.
- **Naturalistic appeal** – "natural/organic" read as "good for you" (even organic cigarettes).
- **Weaselers** – qualifiers that defuse exaggeration ("lose **up to** 17 pounds").
- **Meaningless statements** – "people don't realize that X is the best-selling product" is a claim about *people*, not the product.
- **Statistical persuasion** – "four out of five dentists recommend…", often unrepresentative and an instance of **suppressed evidence**.
- **Subliminal messaging** and **product placement** (ET's Reese's Pieces sales spike, the T-Mobile ringtone, Coke cups on *American Idol*, GM cars in *Transformers*).
- **Packaging tricks** – "gold foil" packaging and idealized **food photography** that outshines the real product.
- **Jingles** – catchy, memorable tunes that keep the brand in your head.
- **Outright lies / empty promises** – false advertising and exaggeration ("twice as fast, half the price").

### In-person sales tricks

- **Reciprocity** – we feel obliged to give back, so free samples, free trials, or picking up a client's tab induce purchases.
- **Benevolence** – gifts that seem to benefit the recipient (free mailing labels, free trial magazine issues for teachers).

### Supermarket psychology

Store layout is engineered to **maximize spending**:

- Easy **entrance**, harder **exit**; the longer you stay, the more you buy.
- **Fresh produce up front** to create relaxation, trust, and a sense of health.
- **Color cues** – red grabs attention, green signals freshness, blue evokes trust.
- Major items along the **perimeter**, with shoppers steered **counterclockwise**; **end-of-aisle displays** and center-aisle placement lure you deeper.
- **Eye-level shelving** (we scan left-to-right) for premium products.
- **Impulse buying** (~70% of decisions made on the spot); the slow **checkout area** stocked with gum, magazines, and knick-knacks. Lesson: **don't shop hungry**.

### Fallacies common in ads

Many techniques are **informal fallacies** (covered later): **scare tactics** and other emotional appeals; **questionable cause** (buy this and something wonderful happens); **popular** and **snob appeal**; **inappropriate authority**; **hasty generalization / stereotypes**; **amphibology** (vague grammar, e.g. "lowest fare guaranteed"); rhetorical devices like **euphemism** ("charming residence"), **hyperbole** ("breakfast of champions"), and empty **slogans** ("obey your thirst"); **faulty analogy** ("like a rock"); **special pleading / suppressed evidence** (the Ford Pinto ad omitting the gas-tank flaw); and **statistical vagueness** ("recommended by 25,000 medical professionals").

### Key takeaway

The skill the course aims to build is to **slow down and evaluate** the information an ad presents, separating genuine claims from the false, the vague, and the merely emotional, so you can enjoy the craft of advertising without being manipulated by it.
