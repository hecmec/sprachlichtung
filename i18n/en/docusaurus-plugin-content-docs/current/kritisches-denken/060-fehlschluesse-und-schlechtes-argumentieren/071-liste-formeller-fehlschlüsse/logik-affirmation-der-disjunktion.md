<!--t src=a851890f-->
:::tip In brief
From „A or B“ and „A is true“ one wrongly concludes „B is false“.

_The inclusive „or“ also permits both._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->
## Definition

<!--t src=6525cec9-->
**Affirming a disjunct** infers, from a disjunction („A or B“) and the truth of one of the two members, the falsity of the other.

<!--t src=1b0da21f-->
The error lies in the fact that the logical „or“ is **inclusive**: „A or B“ means „at least one of the two“, hence possibly both at once. The truth of A therefore does not entail the falsity of B.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->
The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=ab19672e-->
1. A or B (at least one).
2. A is true.
3. Therefore B is not true.

<!--t src=94a3c1a4-->
The inference would be valid only if an **exclusive** „either … or“ were explicitly assumed, or as a _disjunctive syllogism_ with a negated member (1. A or B. 2. Not A. 3. Therefore B.).

<!--t src=30e00fef-->
Also known as: _affirming a disjunct_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->
## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->

<!--t src=b64d9647-->

- **False dilemma** – related: there too an inclusive or incomplete „or“ is treated like a strict either-or.

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=e0982e10-->
> 1. Lena is a doctor or an artist.
> 2. Lena is a doctor.
> 3. Therefore Lena is not an artist.

<!--t src=45e5927b-->
Lena can be both a doctor _and_ an artist. The truth of one member does not exclude the other.

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=38f37b0c-->
> „For dessert there is cake or ice cream. There is cake – so no ice cream.“

<!--t src=b980e9e5-->
As long as „either … or“ is not explicitly meant, there can also be both.  
The inference is not valid.  
If you ask the host nicely, she will surely give you both cake _and_ ice cream, and perhaps even a coffee as well.
