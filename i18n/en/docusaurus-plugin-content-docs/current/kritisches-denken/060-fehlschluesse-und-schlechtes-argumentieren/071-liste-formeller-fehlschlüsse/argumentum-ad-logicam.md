<!--t src=ce122ce4-->
:::tip In brief
This argument is invalid, therefore its conclusion is false.

_Badly reasoned, therefore false._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->
## Definition

<!--t src=c6cdf154-->
The **Argumentum ad Logicam** means, roughly, „argument from logic“.
It is a fallacy that infers the falsity of a claim from the poor reasons given for it. But an invalid argument may perfectly well have a true claim as its subject.

<!--t src=5084769d-->
The error lies in confusing the **argument** with the **conclusion**: that someone argues badly for a claim only shows that _this one_ argument is worthless. It says nothing about the truth value of the claim itself. The claim might be true for entirely different reasons.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->
The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=f4bcad24-->
1. Person A holds claim B and supports it with argument X.
2. Argument X is flawed (invalid or untenable).
3. Therefore claim B is false.

<!--t src=8c19527a-->
The step from (2) to (3) is illegitimate: a bad argument only entails that B is _not proven_, not that B is _false_.

<!--t src=399e95ca-->
### Politics

<!--t src=a5803309-->
This fallacy is often met with in politics.  
Suppose you lean politically left of centre (if that means anything). Now a right-wing politician says: "We need higher import duties." And you tell yourself: "these stupid right-wing politicians talk nothing but nonsense (have bad arguments), so their conclusion can only be false."
Whether you lean right or left, you have just fallen for the argumentum ad logicam.

<!--t src=224c4f7e-->
Also known as: _fallacy fallacy_ / _argument from fallacy_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->
## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=acd733e2-->
- **Non sequitur** – the argumentum ad logicam is ultimately a non sequitur: „the argument is flawed“ simply does not entail „the claim is false“.
- **Genetic fallacy** – related, in that here too a claim is judged not by its content but by something external (its origin or its justification).
- **Straw man** – often combined with the argumentum ad logicam: one refutes a weak (or distorted) justification and thereby declares the actual thesis settled.

## Examples

<!--t src=d4da0067-->
### Example 1: Tigers and cats

<!--t src=2e7bdbb8-->
A flawed syllogism states the following on two levels:

<!--t src=c431c4dc-->
> Here is a logical inference
> All cats are animals.
> All tigers are animals.
> It follows that: all tigers are cats.

<!--t src=cba05094-->
> This inference is invalid.
> Therefore tigers are not cats.

<!--t src=34c14eea-->
This classic fallacy is called the "fallacy of the undistributed middle".  
Whatever it is called, the inference really is invalid – yet tigers are still not cats for all that.

<!--t src=87c37814-->
### Example 2: Authority and truth

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=d3948c66-->
> – „The Earth is round, because my teacher said so.“
> – „That is an appeal to authority, hence a fallacy. Therefore the Earth is _not_ round.“

<!--t src=10aad1e5-->
The justification („my teacher said so“) really is a weak argument. But to conclude from this that the Earth is not round is the argumentum ad logicam: the Earth is still round, just for other reasons.

<!--t src=8c8e7fc2-->
### Example 3: Smoking and grandmother

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=06caf423-->
> – „Smoking is unhealthy, because my grandmother always said so.“
> – „That is merely an anecdotal and traditional argument. So smoking is not unhealthy at all.“

<!--t src=b089221e-->
Here too the argument put forward is weak. But the harmfulness of smoking is well established by extensive scientific evidence. One person's poor attempt at justification changes nothing about that.
