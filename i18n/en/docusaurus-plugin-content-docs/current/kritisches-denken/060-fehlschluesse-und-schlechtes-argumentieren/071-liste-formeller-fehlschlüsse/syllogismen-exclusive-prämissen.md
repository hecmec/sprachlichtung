<!--t src=fc4ff186-->
:::tip In brief
From two negative premises no valid conclusion can be drawn.

_Two separations make no connection._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->
## Definition

<!--t src=bc34ac49-->
A **rule of the categorical syllogism** states: from **two negative premises** nothing follows. If a conclusion is nevertheless drawn, the fallacy of „exclusive premises“ is present.

<!--t src=5081d1dd-->
The reason: each negative premise only says that the middle term is *separated* from an end term. If **both** premises are negative, the middle term is separated from both end terms – it can then build no bridge between them. Everything about the relation of the end terms to one another remains open.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->
The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=43bac51e-->
1. No $M$ is $P$. (negative)
2. Some $S$ are not $M$. (negative)
3. Therefore some $S$ are not $P$. (or any other conclusion)

<!--t src=c79ec624-->
None of the possible conclusions follows from the two premises.

<!--t src=ea724da0-->
Also known as: _exclusive premises_ / _fallacy of exclusive premises_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->
## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=00ce85ce-->
- **Affirmative conclusion from a negative premise** – a related violation of the syllogism's quality rules.
- **Negative conclusion from affirmative premises** – the counterpart on the affirmative side.
- **Undistributed middle** – likewise a case in which the middle term fails to connect the end terms.

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=c5b518ba-->
> 1. No reptile is a mammal.
> 2. Some pets are not mammals.
> 3. Therefore some pets are reptiles.

<!--t src=bd7ad292-->
Both premises are negative. They only say what is *not* connected; about the relation of pets and reptiles they say nothing. The conclusion is not supported.

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=d1476d91-->
> 1. No noble gas reacts with water.
> 2. Hydrogen is not a noble gas.
> 3. Therefore hydrogen reacts with water.

<!--t src=ea511def-->
From two negations nothing follows. That hydrogen is not a noble gas says nothing about its reactivity.
