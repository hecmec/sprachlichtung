:::tip In brief
From „not (A and B)“ and „not A“ one wrongly concludes „B“.

_„Not both“ leaves open that neither holds._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=c9c3b5f5-->

**Denying a conjunct** infers, from the negation of a conjunction („not both A and B“) and the falsity of one member, the truth of the other.

<!--t src=c1519886-->

The error lies in the fact that „not (A and B)“ only rules out that _both hold at once_. It leaves open that _neither_ of the two holds. From „not A“, therefore, „B“ does not follow.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=c8e599c7-->

1. Not (A and B). — It is not the case that A and B both hold.
2. A is not true.
3. Therefore B is true.

<!--t src=316423d4-->

Also known as: _denying a conjunct_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=84b213a2-->

> 1. It is not the case that Paul is rich and happy.
> 2. Paul is not rich.
> 3. Therefore Paul is happy.

<!--t src=cfeb6f3f-->

Paul could also be neither rich nor happy. That both do not hold at once says nothing about whether the one alone holds.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=9eadcba2-->

> „She cannot be in Berlin and in Munich at the same time. She is not in Berlin &mdash; so she is in Munich.“

<!--t src=15ee003a-->

She could just as well be in Hamburg. Ruling out simultaneous presence permits no inference to a particular place.
