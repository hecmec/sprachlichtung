:::tip In brief
From a negative premise an affirmative conclusion is drawn.

_An exclusion cannot ground a membership._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=89fcd2d8-->

A **rule of the categorical syllogism** states: if **one of the premises is negative**, the conclusion must also be negative. If an **affirmative** conclusion is nevertheless drawn, the fallacy of an „affirmative conclusion from a negative premise“ (also _illicit negative_) is present.

<!--t src=0406d185-->

The reason: a negative premise separates two classes from one another. Such a separation can never lead to an affirmative conclusion, which after all asserts a membership.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=33d027dd-->

1. No $M$ is $P$. (negative)
2. All $S$ are $M$. (affirmative)
3. Therefore some $S$ are also $P$. (affirmative)

<!--t src=eddf36de-->

Validly, only the negative conclusion „No $S$ is $P$“ follows from these premises.

<!--t src=520d605c-->

Also known as: _illicit negative_ / _affirmative conclusion from a negative premise_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=8cf6b666-->

> 1. No fish is a mammal.
> 2. All whales are mammals.
> 3. Therefore some whales are fish.

<!--t src=4468afb1-->

The negative premise separates fish from mammals. From it no affirmative conclusion can follow. Validly, only „No whale is a fish“ follows.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=450afe1d-->

> 1. No pacifist carries a weapon.
> 2. All soldiers carry a weapon.
> 3. Therefore some soldiers are pacifists.

<!--t src=814d106a-->

Here too, from a separation (negative) a membership (affirmative) is derived &mdash; formally inadmissible. Validly, only „No soldier is a pacifist“ follows.
