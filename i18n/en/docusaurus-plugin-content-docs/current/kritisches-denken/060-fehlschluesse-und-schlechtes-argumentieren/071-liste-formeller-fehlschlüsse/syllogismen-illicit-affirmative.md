:::tip In brief
From two affirmative premises a negative conclusion is drawn.

_Where nothing is excluded, nothing can be excluded._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=7c51d1b8-->
One **rule of the categorical syllogism** is: if **both premises are affirmative**, then the conclusion must also be affirmative. If a **negative** conclusion is nevertheless drawn, the fallacy of the "negative conclusion from affirmative premises" (also _illicit affirmative_) is present.

<!--t src=30b6008e-->

The reason: a negative conclusion asserts that two classes (wholly or partly) **exclude** one another. Affirmative premises, however, only establish inclusions and can never ground such an exclusion.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=569f8213-->

1. All $M$ are $P$. (affirmative)
2. All $S$ are $M$. (affirmative)
3. Therefore some $S$ are **not** $P$. (negative)

<!--t src=6c8ab805-->

Validly, only the affirmative conclusion „All $S$ are $P$“ follows from these premises.

<!--t src=9c63e523-->

Also known as: _illicit affirmative_ / _negative conclusion from affirmative premises_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=a524ba03-->

> 1. All cats are mammals.
> 2. All mammals are animals.
> 3. Therefore some animals are not cats.

<!--t src=3e87ce0c-->
The conclusion is indeed _materially_ true, but does not follow: from two affirmative statements no negation can be obtained. Validly, only "All cats are animals" follows.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=8dea6764-->

> 1. All roses are plants.
> 2. All plants are living things.
> 3. Therefore some living things are not roses.

<!--t src=eac1e5aa-->
Here too, from pure inclusion (affirmative) an exclusion (negative) is asserted &mdash; this is formally inadmissible.
