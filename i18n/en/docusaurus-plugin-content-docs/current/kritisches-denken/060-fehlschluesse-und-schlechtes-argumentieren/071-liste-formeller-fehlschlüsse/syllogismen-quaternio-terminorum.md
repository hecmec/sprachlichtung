<!--t src=2f2ecdaf-->
:::tip In brief
A syllogism seemingly contains three terms but in truth four – usually through an ambiguous word.

_One word, two meanings, no valid inference._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->
## Definition

<!--t src=6586ade7-->
A valid categorical syllogism consists of exactly **three** terms: two end terms and a middle term that connects them. In the **Quaternio Terminorum** (Latin „fourness of terms“) there are in fact **four** terms.

<!--t src=a085eff8-->
Most often the fourth term arises through **equivocation**: a word is used in the two premises with *different meanings*. The supposed middle term is then no longer a shared term at all and cannot link the premises.

<!--t src=80c36cc3-->
The fallacy has the following form ($M_1$, $M_2$ = the same word in two meanings):

<!--t src=bb3f08f4-->
1. All $M_1$ are $P$.
2. All $S$ are $M_2$.
3. Therefore all $S$ are also $P$.

<!--t src=ae175710-->
Since $M_1$ and $M_2$ are different terms, the connecting middle is missing.

<!--t src=f4f56606-->
Also known as: _four-term fallacy_ / _fallacy of four terms_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->
## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=54802f70-->
- **Equivocation (ambiguity fallacy)** – the informal root: the fourth term arises through an ambiguous word.
- **Undistributed middle** – a related structural error of the syllogism, there through an undistributed rather than a doubled middle term.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->
## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=d84c1a8b-->
> 1. A feather is light. *(German „leicht“: not heavy)*
> 2. What is „leicht“ is easy to understand. *(German „leicht“: easy)*
> 3. Therefore a feather is easy to understand.

<!--t src=2564de16-->
„Leicht“ means in premise 1 *not heavy (in weight)*, in premise 2 *easy*. The word only seems to connect the sentences – there are in fact four terms.

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=c6a2a6f6-->
> 1. All stars shine in the night sky.
> 2. Some film actors are stars.
> 3. Therefore some film actors shine in the night sky.

<!--t src=bfb502cc-->
„Star“ means once a *celestial body*, once a *famous person*. The two meanings are different terms.
