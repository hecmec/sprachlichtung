:::tip In brief
„If A, then B“ is wrongly equated with „If B, then A“.

_The converse of a true conditional need not be true._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=a4a1ac98-->

The **converse error** treats the statement „If A, then B“ as if it were equivalent to its converse „If B, then A“. In fact the two statements are not logically equivalent.

<!--t src=4a5d302f-->

The error confuses a _sufficient_ condition with a _necessary_ one: that A suffices for B does not mean that A is required for B. Logically equivalent to „If A, then B“ is only the contrapositive „If not B, then not A“ &mdash; not the converse.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=ad86f351-->

1. If A, then B.
2. Therefore: If B, then A.

<!--t src=7b2507ef-->

When this illegitimate conversion is applied to a concrete case, the result is **affirming the consequent**.

<!--t src=d3f42e7f-->

Also known as: _converse error_ / _illicit conversion_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=aca86fb2-->

- **Affirming the consequent** &mdash; the applied form of the same confusion.
- **Denying the antecedent** &mdash; related: here too necessary and sufficient conditions are swapped.
- **Non sequitur** &mdash; the umbrella term.

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=3558cdaf-->

> 1. If an animal is a dog, then it is a mammal.
> 2. Therefore: If an animal is a mammal, then it is a dog.

<!--t src=27086d4b-->

Cats, whales and humans are also mammals. The converse is obviously false, even though the original conditional is true.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=1aaf71b5-->

> „All successful entrepreneurs get up early. So whoever gets up early will become a successful entrepreneur.“

<!--t src=be46d278-->

Getting up early may be common among the successful, but it is no guarantee of success. The converse of the statement does not hold.
