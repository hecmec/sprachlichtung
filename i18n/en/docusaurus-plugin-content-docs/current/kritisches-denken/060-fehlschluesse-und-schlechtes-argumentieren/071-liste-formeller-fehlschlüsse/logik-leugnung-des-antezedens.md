:::tip In brief
From „If A, then B“ and „A is false“ one wrongly concludes „B is false“.

_No cause, hence no effect &mdash; although the effect can come about otherwise._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=ff7ccaa8-->

**Denying the antecedent** infers, from a conditional and the falsity of its antecedent, the falsity of its consequent.

<!--t src=8a642f50-->

The error lies in the fact that A is only a _sufficient_, not a _necessary_, condition for B. B can also occur without A.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=1e30b8e7-->

1. If A, then B.
2. A is not true.
3. Therefore B is not true.

<!--t src=2308bda2-->

Valid, by contrast, is only the _modus tollens_ (1. If A, then B. 2. Not B. 3. Therefore not A.).

<!--t src=7ee8af2b-->

Also known as: _denying the antecedent_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=e8413533-->

- **Affirming the consequent** &mdash; the mirror image: there one infers from the affirmed consequent to the affirmed antecedent.
- **Converse error** &mdash; related, because both confuse necessary and sufficient conditions.
- **Non sequitur** &mdash; the umbrella term for all these invalid inferences.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=0a4ddd51-->

> 1. If I am in Vienna, I am in Austria.
> 2. I am not in Vienna.
> 3. Therefore I am not in Austria.

<!--t src=98f50463-->

One can also be in Graz, Linz or Salzburg and thus in Austria nonetheless. From „not in Vienna“, „not in Austria“ does not follow.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=0a86d5a8-->

> 1. If someone has a fever, they are ill.
> 2. Max has no fever.
> 3. Therefore Max is not ill.

<!--t src=0cb87172-->

One can also be ill without a fever (e.g. with a broken bone). The absence of the condition does not rule out the illness.
