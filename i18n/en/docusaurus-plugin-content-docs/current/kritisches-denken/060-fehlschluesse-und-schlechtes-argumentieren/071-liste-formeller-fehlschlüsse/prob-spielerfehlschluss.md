:::tip In brief
With independent random events, the next outcome is inferred from past ones.

_Chance has no memory._
:::

## Definition

<!--t src=ce79bb23-->
The **gambler's fallacy** is the assumption that, with mutually **independent** random events, a particular outcome becomes „more due“ or „less likely“ depending on how often it has recently occurred.

<!--t src=54303a46-->
The error fails to recognise the **independence** of the events: a fair coin has, on every toss, the unchanged probability $P = 0{,}5$ for „heads“ – regardless of what came before. The probability of long runs is indeed small, but after a run that has already occurred the next toss is again a new, uninfluenced event.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->
The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=3aab29ee-->
1. The event E has occurred (or failed to occur) several times in a row.
2. E and the earlier outcomes are in truth independent.
3. Therefore E is less (or more) likely next time.

<!--t src=858927e1-->
Formally, the inference confuses the prior probability of a whole run with the **conditional** probability of the next toss: $P(\text{heads} \mid \text{5× tails before}) = 0{,}5$.

<!--t src=a889025c-->
Also known as: _gambler's fallacy_

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=3fe24cb8-->
- **Hot-hand fallacy** – the opposite direction: from a run one infers its continuation („on a roll, stays on a roll“).
- **Inverse gambler's fallacy** – to be distinguished: from a rare single outcome one (wrongly) concludes that many attempts must have preceded it.
- **Base rate fallacy** – related as a typical error in dealing with probabilities.
- **Representativeness heuristic** – the cognitive root: short runs are supposed to „look random“.

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=3f083bba-->
> At roulette, red has come up five times in a row. A player now bets heavily on black, because black is „now overdue“.

<!--t src=bfc92709-->
The ball does not know the previous rounds. The probability of black is unchanged in the next game (in European roulette $18/37$).

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=d7cbab03-->
> „We have already had three daughters – the next child will surely be a boy.“

<!--t src=d9921f23-->
The sex of each child is approximately independent of the previous ones. The prior history does not increase the probability of a boy.
