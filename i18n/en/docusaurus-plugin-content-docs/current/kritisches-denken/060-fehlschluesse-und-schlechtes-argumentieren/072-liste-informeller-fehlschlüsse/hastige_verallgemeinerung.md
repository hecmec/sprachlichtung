:::tip In brief
From few or untypical cases a general rule is made.

_From the sample, prematurely to the whole._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=9ce3770f-->

In the **hasty generalisation**, from a **sample that is too small or unrepresentative** a **general rule** is inferred.

<!--t src=d00e5b49-->

The error: a reliable generalisation needs a sufficiently large and typical selection. From few, perhaps randomly picked cases no general validity can be obtained. This is how prejudices and stereotypes arise.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=27605d53-->

1. Some (few) A have the property E.
2. Therefore all A have the property E.

<!--t src=534aa7fa-->

Also known as: _hasty generalization_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=f3c58cbb-->

- **Cherry picking** &mdash; related: the selection of cases is not representative.
- **False analogy** &mdash; related: inadmissible transfer to other cases.
- **Accident** &mdash; the counterpart (_converse accident_): from the general rule to the special case.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=ee7bc2be-->

> „Two tourists from that country were rude &mdash; the people there are just all rude.“

<!--t src=1b30d81f-->

Two cases are no adequate basis for a judgement about millions of people. This is how stereotypes arise.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=a8d9bf75-->

> „My grandfather smoked and lived to 95 &mdash; so smoking can't be harmful.“

<!--t src=9876e1e9-->

A single case does not refute a statistic. From it no general rule can be derived.
