:::tip In brief
Something is held to be true or right because many believe or do it.

_Millions of flies can't be wrong?_
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=10b0f4c2-->

In the **Argumentum ad Populum** (appeal to the majority, _bandwagon_), a claim counts as true or an action as right **because many** agree with it or act that way.

<!--t src=9ae7e5e0-->

The error: the prevalence of an opinion is no evidence of its truth. Majorities have often been wrong. Popularity and correctness are two different things.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=1d93dd9b-->

1. The majority believes B (or does B).
2. Therefore B is true (or right).

<!--t src=dad13af1-->

Also known as: _appeal to the people_ / _bandwagon_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=1fe2a78a-->

- **Argumentum ad Verecundiam** &mdash; related: appeal to a supposed authority instead of to the crowd.
- **Argumentum ad Antiquitatem** &mdash; related: not „many“, but „long done this way“.
- **Argumentum ad Baculum** &mdash; related, when the majority turns into social pressure.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=fe456a71-->

> „This can't be wrong &mdash; millions of people use it, after all.“

<!--t src=c0acfaa0-->

How many people use something says nothing about whether it is any good. Even widely used products or views can be bad or false.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=115b605b-->

> „Everyone knows that …“

<!--t src=22405ae2-->

The reference to supposed common knowledge replaces the argument that ought actually to support the claim.
