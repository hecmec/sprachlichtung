<!--t src=5473f68f-->
:::tip In brief
A necessary condition is taken for a sufficient one – or vice versa.

_Necessary does not mean enough._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->
## Definition

<!--t src=ea2c5377-->
In this fallacy, **necessary** and **sufficient** conditions are confused.

<!--t src=f7789df8-->
- A **necessary** condition must be met *for* something to *be able* to occur (without it, not).
- A **sufficient** condition is enough on its own *for* something to occur (with it, always).

<!--t src=eee83bf5-->
The error: „A is necessary for B“ is wrongly turned into „A is enough for B“ (or vice versa). The two are different: oxygen is *necessary* for fire, but not *sufficient* – otherwise everything would be burning.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->
The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=48289104-->
1. A is a necessary condition for B.
2. A is present.
3. Therefore B occurs. (inadmissible: A is not enough)

<!--t src=c240f4f7-->
In propositional logic this corresponds to **affirming the consequent** or the **converse error**.

<!--t src=70ada299-->
Also known as: _confusing necessary and sufficient conditions_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->
## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=bd7cd6f4-->
- **Affirming the consequent** – the formal counterpart in propositional logic.
- **Converse error** – related: „If A, then B“ is treated like „If B, then A“.
- **Single-cause thinking** – related, when a necessary factor is taken for the whole cause.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->
## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=624baa58-->
> „Whoever passes the exam has studied. You have studied – so you'll pass.“

<!--t src=e26c35f1-->
Studying is (perhaps) *necessary*, but not *sufficient*. One can study and still fail.

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=665cdf21-->
> „Money is necessary to be happy. I have money – so I am happy.“

<!--t src=98f8746d-->
Even if money were a necessary condition, it would not be enough. The necessary condition is confused with a sufficient one.
