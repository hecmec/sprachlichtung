<!--t src=744aa5eb-->
:::tip In brief
Because B followed A, A is taken to be the cause of B.

_After it does not mean because of it._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->
## Definition

<!--t src=2a4c03ad-->
In **post hoc ergo propter hoc** (Latin „after this, therefore because of this“), from the mere **temporal succession** of two events a **causal** connection is inferred: because B happened after A, A is supposed to be the cause of B.

<!--t src=28b7997a-->
The error: succession is not causation. B may follow by chance, a third cause may bring about both, or the connection may be the other way round. The closely related variant **cum hoc ergo propter hoc** correspondingly infers cause from mere *simultaneity* (correlation).

<!--t src=3c24b250-->
The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=4fcdb769-->
1. Event A happened before event B.
2. Therefore A caused event B.

<!--t src=7ec66ba8-->
Also known as: _post hoc ergo propter hoc_ / _false cause_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->
## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=18bd8815-->
- **Cum hoc ergo propter hoc** – the sister variant: simultaneity instead of succession.
- **Single-cause thinking** – related: premature commitment to one cause.
- **Teleological fallacy** – related: reading connections into things.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->
## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=68060115-->
> „After the new government took office, crime rose – so the government is to blame.“

<!--t src=9eb937be-->
The temporal succession is no evidence of a cause. The rise can have quite different reasons.

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=b5c4269c-->
> „I had this lucky charm with me and then passed the exam – so it works.“

<!--t src=0ea9fa11-->
That the passing followed does not make the lucky charm its cause. Here it was the studying that worked, not the talisman.
