<!--t src=1922f83e-->
:::tip In brief
Agreement is achieved by arousing pity instead of by substantive reasons.

_Pity proves nothing._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->
## Definition

<!--t src=f3974669-->
In the **Argumentum ad Misericordiam** (appeal to pity), instead of substantive reasons the **compassion** of the other party is addressed in order to push through a claim or demand.

<!--t src=4205c9d6-->
The error: pity is an understandable feeling, but no evidence. Whether a claim is true or a demand justified does not depend on how sorry we feel for the person concerned. (Compassion can be morally *relevant* – it becomes a fallacy when it replaces a *substantive* piece of evidence.)

<!--t src=3c24b250-->
The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=0f5c9db5-->
1. Person A is in a pitiable situation.
2. Therefore A's claim (or demand) B is justified.

<!--t src=9e07f4bb-->
Also known as: _appeal to pity_ / _argumentum ad misericordiam_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->
## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=925afec3-->
- **Appeal to emotion** – the umbrella term: here compassion is addressed.
- **Argumentum ad Baculum** – the counterpart, through fear instead of pity.

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=cedc19a0-->
> „Professor, you have to give me a better grade – I studied all night and urgently need the scholarship.“

<!--t src=49cd0535-->
However understandable the situation: it says nothing about the quality of the exam performance, which is what the grade is about.

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=db81ff0f-->
> „Just let him win this once, he has already had such a hard life.“

<!--t src=034fe2fa-->
The person's fate is no reason that settles the question of who actually played better.
