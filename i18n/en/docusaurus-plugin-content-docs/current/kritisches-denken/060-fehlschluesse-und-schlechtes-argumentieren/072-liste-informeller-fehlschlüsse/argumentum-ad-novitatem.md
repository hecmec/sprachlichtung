<!--t src=1bf15d0c-->
:::tip In brief
Something counts as better merely because it is new or modern.

_New does not mean better._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->
## Definition

<!--t src=3b50374a-->
In the **Argumentum ad Novitatem** (appeal to novelty), something is held to be better or more correct **because it is new** or more modern.

<!--t src=13a00c60-->
The error: novelty alone is no evidence of quality. Much that is new is immature, and some tried-and-tested things remain superior. It is the mirror-image counterpart to the **Argumentum ad Antiquitatem**.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->
The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=44694f45-->
1. B is new (or newer than the alternative).
2. Therefore B is better/correct.

<!--t src=35381686-->
Also known as: _appeal to novelty_ / _argumentum ad novitatem_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->
## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=0b8ced83-->
- **Argumentum ad Antiquitatem** – the counterpart: „old, hence proven“.
- **Argumentum ad Populum** – related, when the new is at the same time regarded as the fashionable.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->
## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=c7fdc1fc-->
> „This is the latest model, so it is the best choice.“

<!--t src=7b01170d-->
Whether it is the best choice depends on its properties and suitability – not on its release date.

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=12edcc1b-->
> „This therapy is brand new, therefore it is superior to the old methods.“

<!--t src=2d5bdd61-->
Precisely new procedures are often still little tested. Novelty is no substitute for proof of efficacy.
