:::tip In brief
A claim counts as true because an (often unsuitable) authority holds it.

_A big name is no proof._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->
## Definition

<!--t src=d81fbf2a-->
In the **Argumentum ad Verecundiam** (appeal to authority), a claim is held to be true solely because an **authority** utters it – often an authority that is *not competent* or *not reliable* for the topic.

<!--t src=7630fa4f-->
Important: relying on relevant experts is *not* a fallacy but reasonable. It becomes a fallacy when (a) the authority is outside their field, (b) there is no consensus within the field, or (c) bare authority is meant to *replace a substantive argument*.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->
The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=d40e3414-->
1. Authority A asserts B.
2. A is no relevant/reliable authority for B.
3. Therefore B is true.

<!--t src=1bc51e25-->
Also known as: _appeal to authority_ / _argumentum ad verecundiam_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->
## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=0ff77e0c-->
- **Argumentum ad Populum** – related: the crowd instead of a single authority.
- **Genetic fallacy** – related: judging by source rather than by content.
- **Argumentum ad Antiquitatem** – related: the „authority“ of tradition.

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=9077bc33-->
> „A famous actor recommends this dietary supplement – so it must be good.“

<!--t src=dcec14cc-->
Skill at acting establishes no medical competence. The person's celebrity says nothing about the effect of the supplement.

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=5c94e520-->
> „A Nobel laureate in physics doubts climate change, so the matter is open.“

<!--t src=ba2b88b4-->
Even a distinguished physicist is not automatically an expert on climate research. What is decisive is the state of research in the relevant field, not a prominent individual name.
