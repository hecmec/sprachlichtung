:::tip In brief
A claim or thing is judged solely by its origin rather than by its content.

_Where something comes from says nothing about whether it is true._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=8d349260-->

The **genetic fallacy** judges a claim, idea or thing by its **origin** &mdash; who invented it, where it comes from, how it arose &mdash; instead of by its actual content.

<!--t src=64cedc28-->

The error: the origin of an idea is irrelevant to its correctness or value. An idea with a dubious origin can be true, one with a venerable origin can be false.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=292ce18a-->

1. B comes from the source Q.
2. Q is (dis)reputable.
3. Therefore B is false (or true).

<!--t src=e7174d30-->

Also known as: _genetic fallacy_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=48695509-->

- **Argumentum ad Hominem** &mdash; a special form: the source is a person.
- **Poisoning the well** &mdash; a special form: a discrediting attribution of origin made in advance.
- **Argumentum ad Antiquitatem** &mdash; the positive variant: origin in tradition taken as a supposed merit.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=63a61dd6-->

> „The motorway network was expanded under the National Socialists &mdash; how can you think motorways are a good thing?“

<!--t src=2a9784b6-->

The historical origin does not decide the present-day usefulness or uselessness of motorways. Origin is no substantive argument.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=55222eab-->

> „This idea came to him in a dream &mdash; so it cannot be taken seriously.“

<!--t src=9e92c644-->

How an idea arose says nothing about its correctness. Even a sudden inspiration can turn out to be true.
