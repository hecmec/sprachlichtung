<!--t src=1b8f335c-->

:::tip In brief
Something is held to be true because one wishes it to be true.

_Wishing does not make it true._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=50afa84a-->

In **wishful thinking**, it is not the facts but the **wish** that determines what someone holds to be true. The agreeableness of an idea is confused with its truth.

<!--t src=cb17f9a3-->

The error: that a statement is _agreeable_ or _desirable_ says nothing about its _correctness_. Closely related is the appeal to consequences, in which from the (un)desirable consequences of a thesis its truth value is inferred.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=cde8bbe5-->

1. I wish that B were true.
2. Therefore B is true.

<!--t src=8984e556-->

Also known as: _wishful thinking_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=db803ff7-->

- **Appeal to emotion** &mdash; related: a feeling (here the wish) replaces the evidence.
- **Personal incredulity** &mdash; the negative counterpart: „I won't/can't believe that, so it's false.“
- **Appeal to consequences** &mdash; related: inferring a thesis's truth value from its consequences.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=fc4bc2c4-->

> „My ticket will surely win &mdash; I just need the money too badly.“

<!--t src=bf6d470b-->

The urgency of the need does not change the probability of winning.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=f4156344-->

> „Climate change can't be that bad, because otherwise we'd have to change our lives fundamentally.“

<!--t src=9a7365b8-->

How inconvenient a consequence would be does not decide whether the underlying fact is true.
