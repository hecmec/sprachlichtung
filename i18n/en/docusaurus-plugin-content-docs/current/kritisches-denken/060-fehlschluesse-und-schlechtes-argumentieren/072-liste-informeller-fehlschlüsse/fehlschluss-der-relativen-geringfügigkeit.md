<!--t src=ff3b5120-->
:::tip In brief
A contribution or harm is declared irrelevant by pointing to its small size.

_„That little bit“ adds up._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->
## Definition

<!--t src=4098305d-->
In the **appeal to relative triviality**, a problem, contribution or harm is dismissed because it is **small in itself** – whether compared to „something worse“ (*relative privation*) or because the individual case barely counts.

<!--t src=ee4c3451-->
The error: the triviality of a single contribution says nothing about the **sum** or the **principle**. Many small contributions produce large effects; and that there are worse things does not make the smaller one harmless.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->
The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=5e9134c9-->
1. The contribution/harm X is (in itself) small.
2. Therefore X is insignificant / not a problem.
3. (Often:) There are worse things anyway.

<!--t src=f6785c5c-->
Also known as: _appeal to relative triviality_ / _minimization_ (special form: _relative privation_ – „there are worse things“)

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->
## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=7e30c692-->
- **Slippery slope** – the opposite direction: there a small step is dramatised beyond measure.
- **Sorites paradox** – related: the question of when many small things start to count.
- **Red herring** – related, when „there are worse things“ steers away from the topic.

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=63922da0-->
> „One little glass won't hurt.“

<!--t src=150fd778-->
On its own a glass may be little – drunk regularly it adds up. The triviality of the single case conceals the effect of the habit.

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=1c04943b-->
> „Why are you getting worked up about this injustice? Elsewhere people are starving.“

<!--t src=8ab8667c-->
That there are bigger problems does not make the smaller one trivial. Pointing to something worse does not refute the objection.
