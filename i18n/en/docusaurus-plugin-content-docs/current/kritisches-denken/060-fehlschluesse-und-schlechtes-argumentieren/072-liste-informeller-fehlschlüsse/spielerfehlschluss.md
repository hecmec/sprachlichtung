:::tip In brief
With independent random events, the next outcome is inferred from the previous ones.

_The die does not remember._

<!--t src=23594b0b-->
## Definition

<!--t src=738772c3-->
In the **gambler's fallacy**, one believes that with **independent** random events an outcome becomes „more due“ the longer it has failed to appear (or „less likely“ the more often it recently occurred).

<!--t src=4c760773-->
The error: independent events have no memory. A fair die shows the six on every throw with the same probability, no matter how long none came. The small probability of a long run concerns the *prediction in advance*, not the *next* throw after a run that has already occurred.

<!--t src=a515c65a-->
:::note
This fallacy is treated in more detail under the **probabilistic fallacies**.
:::

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=ec7b6d69-->
> „I've now thrown twenty times without a six – now one really has to come.“

<!--t src=9dbd77c8-->
The probability of a six remains $1/6$ on every throw. The run of bad luck so far changes nothing about that.

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=5d42d189-->
> „At roulette red came up five times – now I bet on black, it's overdue.“

<!--t src=e9dd34b2-->
The ball does not know the previous rounds. Black has not become more likely.
