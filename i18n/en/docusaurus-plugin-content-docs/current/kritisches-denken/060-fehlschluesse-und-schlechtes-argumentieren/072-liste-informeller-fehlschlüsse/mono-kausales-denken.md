:::tip In brief
A complex effect is traced back to a single cause.

_One cause, where many act together._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=ff8b33c2-->

In **single-cause thinking** (causal oversimplification), an effect that in truth depends on **several causes** is traced back to a **single** cause. The interplay of factors is blanked out.

<!--t src=4a322a58-->

The error: many phenomena &mdash; social, medical, economic &mdash; arise from a web of causes. Whoever picks out only one oversimplifies inadmissibly and often draws the wrong conclusions.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=dae168c4-->

1. The effect W has the causes U₁, U₂, U₃ …
2. W is traced back to U₁ alone.
3. The remaining causes are ignored.

<!--t src=88b87bac-->

Also known as: _causal oversimplification_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=def5f4f4-->

- **Post hoc ergo propter hoc** &mdash; related: premature attribution of cause.
- **False dilemma** &mdash; related: artificial narrowing of the possibilities.
- **Cherry picking** &mdash; related, when only the one fitting factor is emphasised.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=8c04830a-->

> „Ever since corporal punishment was abolished, children have had no discipline at all.“

<!--t src=df5bc990-->

Discipline depends on many factors (upbringing, role models, school, social environment). To pin it on a single measure is an inadmissible oversimplification.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=2576189d-->

> „He is poor because he is simply too lazy.“

<!--t src=b82b1ddd-->

Poverty as a rule has many causes (education, health, the labour market, background). To pick out a single one distorts the picture.

<!--t src=69b1092f-->
