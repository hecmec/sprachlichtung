:::tip In brief
What holds for the individual parts is transferred without justification to the whole.

_Good parts do not necessarily make a good whole._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=de9d9dce-->
In the **fallacy of composition**, it is assumed that a property of the **parts** automatically applies to the **whole** they form as well.

<!--t src=b96ddcd2-->
The error: some properties carry over from the parts to the whole, but many do not &mdash; precisely where the **interplay** matters. The whole can have properties that no part possesses on its own (emergence).

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=915b75fe-->

1. Every part of W has the property E.
2. Therefore the whole W also has the property E.

<!--t src=b8367af3-->

Also known as: _fallacy of composition_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=15e7fb01-->
- **Fallacy of division** &mdash; the mirror image: from the whole to the parts.
- **Hasty generalization** &mdash; related: an inadmissible transfer to a different level.
- **Ecological fallacy** &mdash; related: inferring from group data to individual data and vice versa.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=6faf9aa6-->
> "Every player on this team is excellent &mdash; so it is an excellent team."

<!--t src=cd3f573b-->

A team can play together poorly despite strong individual players. The property of the part does not carry over automatically to the whole.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=d386de43-->

> „Every ingredient of this dish tastes good on its own, so the dish tastes good.“

<!--t src=b1f2fe6d-->

Good-tasting ingredients do not necessarily add up to a good dish.
