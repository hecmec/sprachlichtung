## Definition

<!--t src=78aa705b-->
In the **straw man** argument, the other party's position is presented in a **distorted, exaggerated or simplified** form, and this easily attackable caricature is then refuted – as if the real position had been refuted.

<!--t src=40c79811-->
The error: what is refuted is not what the person actually holds, but a self-built distortion. The real position remains untouched.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->
The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=ee16cc84-->
1. Person A holds position P.
2. Person B attributes to A the distorted position P′.
3. B refutes P′.
4. B acts as if P had thereby been refuted.

<!--t src=c52edd7a-->
Also known as: _straw man_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->
## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=b2279a07-->
- **Red herring** – related: both evade the real matter.
- **Motte-and-bailey fallacy** – a related game with shifted positions.
- **False dilemma** – related, when the opposing position is narrowed to an extreme variant.

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=c9a9697d-->
> – „We should take in refugees who are in need.“
> – „So you want to bring all the world's misery here and abolish the borders.“

<!--t src=59fededf-->
„Taking in people in need“ is turned into „abolishing all borders“ – an exaggeration that no one held that way.

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=eb0c0191-->
> – „I think children shouldn't watch television all day.“
> – „So you want to forbid children every joy.“

<!--t src=7da146e9-->
The moderate statement is inflated into a ban on „every joy“ and then indignantly rejected.
