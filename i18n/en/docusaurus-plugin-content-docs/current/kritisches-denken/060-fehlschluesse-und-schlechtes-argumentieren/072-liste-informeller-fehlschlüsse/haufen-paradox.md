:::tip In brief
From the vagueness of a term it is concluded that it does not exist at all &mdash; or that every boundary is arbitrary.

_When do grains become a heap?_
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=fcb9b320-->

The **sorites paradox** (from Greek _sorós_ „heap“) arises with **vague terms** without a sharp boundary: a single grain of sand is not a heap; one grain more makes (in each case) no difference &mdash; so a million grains would not be a heap either. Conversely, one can „prove“ that even a single grain is a heap.

<!--t src=4231bca5-->

The error lies in the **continuum fallacy**: from the fact that there is **no sharp boundary**, it is wrongly concluded that there is **no difference at all** (between heap and non-heap, bald and hairy, young and old). The lack of an exact boundary does not abolish the clear cases.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=cc28b207-->

1. A single X makes no difference (no heap / not bald …).
2. Each further step likewise makes no difference.
3. Therefore there is never a difference (no heap / no baldness).

<!--t src=403427c7-->

Also known as: _sorites paradox_ / _continuum fallacy_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=a928b11d-->

- **False dichotomy** &mdash; the counterpart: there a continuum is wrongly _split in two_, here the existence of stages is _denied_.
- **Appeal to relative triviality** &mdash; related: every small step „doesn't count“.

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=0483d346-->

> „Losing one hair makes no one bald. So one never becomes bald through hair loss.“

<!--t src=80b5f121-->

That no single hair marks the boundary does not mean there is no baldness. The inference abuses the vagueness of the term.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=63c416ca-->

> „One cent makes no one rich. So no sum, however large, makes anyone rich either.“

<!--t src=db8db1f4-->

The lack of an exact wealth boundary does not refute that there are rich and poor people.
