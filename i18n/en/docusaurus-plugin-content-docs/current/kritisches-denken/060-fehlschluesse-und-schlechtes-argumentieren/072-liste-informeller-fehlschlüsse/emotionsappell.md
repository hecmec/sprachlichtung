<!--t src=67ba35ef-->

:::tip In brief
Instead of with reasons, a claim is promoted with feelings.

_Feelings persuade, but prove nothing._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=82c34394-->
The **appeal to emotion** replaces substantive reasons with the deliberate arousal of **feelings** &mdash; fear, pity, pride, outrage, hope &mdash; in order to produce agreement.

<!--t src=6f334d02-->
The error: feelings can influence what we _want_ to believe, but they are no evidence of what is _true_. A strong feeling does not make a claim correct. The appeal to emotion is the umbrella term for several named varieties.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=048dc0ea-->

1. The claim B is linked to strong feelings.
2. The audience feels these feelings.
3. Therefore B is accepted.

<!--t src=7b1dbc88-->

Also known as: _appeal to emotion_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=bf3a9a44-->
- **Argumentum ad misericordiam** &mdash; a variety: appeal to pity.
- **Argumentum ad baculum** &mdash; a variety: appeal to fear.
- **Wishful thinking** &mdash; related: the wish replaces the evidence.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=39f57753-->

> „Think of the children! Anyone who is for this law cannot look them in the eye.“

<!--t src=563951bc-->

The appeal to guilt and worry replaces the substantive examination of whether the law actually makes sense.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=2feadaf5-->
> A commercial shows happy families to emotional music &mdash; without a single argument for the product.

<!--t src=19861c8b-->

The good feeling produced is meant to carry the purchasing decision, but says nothing about the quality of the product.
