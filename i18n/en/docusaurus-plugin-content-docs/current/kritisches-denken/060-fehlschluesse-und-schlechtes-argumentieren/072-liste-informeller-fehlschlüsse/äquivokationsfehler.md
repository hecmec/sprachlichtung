:::tip In brief
An ambiguous word is used within one argument in two different meanings.

_The same word, two meanings._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->
## Definition

<!--t src=4b2f4912-->
In **equivocation**, an **ambiguous word** is used within the same argument in **different meanings**. Because the word stays outwardly the same, the inference seems to „fit“ – in truth the sentences speak of different things.

<!--t src=7d1a08b3-->
The error: the cogency rests only on the same wording, not on a shared meaning. In syllogisms this produces the **Quaternio Terminorum** (a fourth term).

<!--t src=3c24b250-->
The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=8ceb1439-->
1. Statement 1 uses the word W in meaning 1.
2. Statement 2 uses the same word W in meaning 2.
3. The inference treats both meanings as one.

<!--t src=cc443331-->
Also known as: _equivocation_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->
## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=5159cbf9-->
- **Quaternio Terminorum** – the formal upshot in the syllogism (four instead of three terms).
- **Ambiguity fallacy (amphiboly)** – related: ambiguity through *sentence structure* instead of through a word.
- **Distinction without a difference** – a related game with meanings.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->
## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=f6a39c2f-->
> 1. A law needs a lawgiver.
> 2. The laws of nature are laws.
> 3. Therefore the laws of nature need a lawgiver.

<!--t src=63d8e19c-->
„Law“ means in 1 a *legal regulation*, in 2 a *scientific regularity*. The two meanings are inadmissibly equated.

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=4f5cb947-->
> „At the end of the tunnel there is light. Light is a form of energy. So at the end of the tunnel there is energy.“

<!--t src=e5ded5a7-->
„Light“ stands first figuratively (for hope, rescue), then physically. The word only seems to connect the sentences.
