:::tip In brief
An ambiguous word is used within one argument in two different meanings.

_The same word, two meanings._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=b4d59ae7-->
In **equivocation**, an **ambiguous word** is used with **different meanings** within the same argument. Because the word outwardly stays the same, the inference seems to "fit" &mdash; in truth the sentences are talking about different things.

<!--t src=7d1a08b3-->

The error: the cogency rests only on the same wording, not on a shared meaning. In syllogisms this produces the **Quaternio Terminorum** (a fourth term).

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=8ceb1439-->

1. Statement 1 uses the word W in meaning 1.
2. Statement 2 uses the same word W in meaning 2.
3. The inference treats both meanings as one.

<!--t src=cc443331-->

Also known as: _equivocation_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=ed8b9c02-->
- **Quaternio terminorum** &mdash; the formal expression in the syllogism (four instead of three terms).
- **Fallacy of ambiguity (amphiboly)** &mdash; related: ambiguity through _sentence structure_ instead of through a word.
- **Distinction without a difference** &mdash; a related game with meanings.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=f6a39c2f-->

> 1. A law needs a lawgiver.
> 2. The laws of nature are laws.
> 3. Therefore the laws of nature need a lawgiver.

<!--t src=997fc67b-->
"Law" means in 1 a _legal regulation_, in 2 a _scientific regularity_. The two meanings are inadmissibly equated.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=4f5cb947-->

> „At the end of the tunnel there is light. Light is a form of energy. So at the end of the tunnel there is energy.“

<!--t src=e5ded5a7-->

„Light“ stands first figuratively (for hope, rescue), then physically. The word only seems to connect the sentences.
