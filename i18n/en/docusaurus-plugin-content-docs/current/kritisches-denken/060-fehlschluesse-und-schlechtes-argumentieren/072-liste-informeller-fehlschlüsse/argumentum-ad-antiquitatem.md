:::tip In brief
Something is taken to be right because it has long been done that way.

_Old does not mean good._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=45ba4693-->

In the **Argumentum ad Antiquitatem** (appeal to tradition), a claim or practice is held to be right **because it is old** or has „always“ been done that way.

<!--t src=8065228b-->

The error: the age of a conviction is no evidence of its correctness. Even long-cultivated customs can be wrong, outdated or harmful. (Conversely, tradition is not automatically wrong &mdash; it is just no _argument_ in itself.)

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=8cda133b-->

1. B has long been believed or practised.
2. Therefore B is right.

<!--t src=98a4c57d-->

Also known as: _appeal to tradition_ / _argumentum ad antiquitatem_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=2f0deca1-->

- **Argumentum ad Novitatem** &mdash; the counterpart: „new, hence better“.
- **Argumentum ad Populum** &mdash; related: not „long“, but „many“.
- **Naturalistic fallacy** &mdash; related: from „this is customary“ to „this is how it ought to be“.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=8aa9f476-->

> „We have always done it this way here &mdash; why should we change it?“

<!--t src=2bc2250b-->

That something has long been the practice says nothing about whether it is the best practice.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=b7f05ae9-->

> „This healing method has been used for centuries, so it works.“

<!--t src=e4c8e126-->

Centuries of use are no substitute for proof of efficacy. Ineffective methods too can persist for a long time.
