## Definition

<!--t src=7c8e01b8-->
The **red herring** (literally a smoked herring, a false trail) leads away from the actual question by introducing an **irrelevant but interesting side issue**. Attention is redirected instead of answering the question at issue.

<!--t src=1ea542f1-->
The error: the side issue may be important in itself, but it has nothing to do with the original question. It is used to dodge an answer.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->
The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=0d6f0e38-->
1. Topic A is up for debate.
2. Instead of A, the distracting topic B is introduced.
3. Attention shifts to B; A remains unanswered.

<!--t src=51b22578-->
Also known as: _red herring_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->
## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=ed4b8624-->
- **Straw man** – related: there the *opposing position* is distorted, here attention is steered to a different *topic*.
- **Tu quoque / whataboutism** – a special form of distraction through counter-accusations.
- **Insinuation** – related: a side issue is merely hinted at.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->
## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->
### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=aa9cd45c-->
> – „Why did you doctor the figures?“
> – „You know, I built this company up from nothing and created hundreds of jobs.“

<!--t src=1d808a4a-->
The achievement of building it up is not the question. It distracts from the actual question about the doctored figures.

<!--t src=f350657d-->
### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=21a01e5d-->
> – „Should we sort our waste better?“
> – „As long as China emits so much CO2, it makes no difference anyway.“

<!--t src=19e3c1d9-->
The reference to China does not answer the question about waste sorting but evades it.
