:::tip In brief
Only the evidence that fits one's own thesis is selected; contradicting evidence is left out.

_Picking out the raisins._
:::

<!--t src=23594b0b-->

## Definition

<!--t src=feefaf83-->

In **cherry picking**, only the data, cases or quotations that **support one's own thesis** are deliberately picked out, while all contradicting evidence is suppressed.

<!--t src=04313ecd-->

The error: a one-sidedly assembled picture can „prove“ almost any thesis. What matters is the _totality_ of the evidence, not a handpicked selection.

<!--t src=3c24b250-->

The fallacy has the following form:

<!--t src=87cc4ab8-->

1. There is evidence for and against the thesis B.
2. Only the confirming evidence is cited.
3. The contradicting evidence is left out.
4. B thereby appears well supported.

<!--t src=4f31456a-->

Also known as: _cherry picking_

<!--t src=2851dcb3-->

## Related fallacies

<!--t src=4d08b756-->
<!-- list similar fallacies -->

<!--t src=083a04cd-->

- **Confirmation bias** &mdash; the cognitive root: we favour confirming information anyway.
- **Hasty generalisation** &mdash; related: a conclusion from an unrepresentative selection.
- **Texas sharpshooter** &mdash; related: picking out apparent patterns after the fact.

<!--t src=9dbba01b-->

## Examples

<!--t src=a67a7a61-->

### Example 1

<!--t src=5422e642-->
<!-- description of example one -->

<!--t src=9bdfd967-->

> „This diet works &mdash; just look at the three people who lost weight on it.“

<!--t src=2cc36dd7-->

The many for whom it did not work go unmentioned. Three selected successes prove nothing about its efficacy.

<!--t src=f350657d-->

### Example 2

<!--t src=c7d6981c-->
<!-- description of example two -->

<!--t src=87662e08-->

> A report cites only the studies that show the desired result and passes over the numerous studies with the opposite finding.

<!--t src=9dbeab54-->

The selection produces a distorted picture of the state of research.
