In formal logic, there are certain patterns of argumentation that are always valid. These patterns form the basis for logically correct reasoning and can be applied in various contexts.

<!--t src=632f8b14-->
## Modus Ponens (Affirming the Antecedent)

<!--t src=3af3d68f-->
**Form:**
1. If A, then B.
2. A.
3. Therefore B.

<!--t src=530bb0b0-->
**Example:**
1. If it rains, the street becomes wet.
2. It is raining.
3. Therefore the street becomes wet.

<!--t src=631c0b37-->
## Modus Tollens (Denying the Consequent)

<!--t src=80f554a9-->
**Form:**
1. If A, then B.
2. Not B.
3. Therefore not A.

<!--t src=b3906386-->
**Example:**
1. If Peter is guilty, there are fingerprints at the crime scene.
2. There are no fingerprints at the crime scene.
3. Therefore Peter is not guilty.

<!--t src=fbebceb7-->
## Hypothetical Syllogism (Chain Argument)

<!--t src=9a71e74d-->
**Form:**
1. If A, then B.
2. If B, then C.
3. Therefore: If A, then C.

<!--t src=1a71440f-->
**Example:**
1. If it rains, the street becomes wet.
2. If the street is wet, it becomes slippery.
3. Therefore: If it rains, the street becomes slippery.

<!--t src=e1d4b6ab-->
## Disjunctive Syllogism (Process of Elimination)

<!--t src=645bda87-->
**Form:**
1. Either A or B.
2. Not A.
3. Therefore B.

<!--t src=597b78b6-->
**Example:**
1. Either Hans broke the window or Maria did.
2. Hans did not break the window.
3. Therefore Maria broke the window.

<!--t src=23f1e918-->
## Conjunctive Simplification

<!--t src=a7d84467-->
**Form:**
1. A and B.
2. Therefore A.

<!--t src=b8d3add4-->
**Example:**
1. It is raining and it is cold.
2. Therefore it is raining.

<!--t src=7fbac6ba-->
## Conjunctive Addition

<!--t src=f02b9d52-->
**Form:**
1. A.
2. B.
3. Therefore A and B.

<!--t src=26fcfe65-->
**Example:**
1. It is raining.
2. It is cold.
3. Therefore it is raining and it is cold.

<!--t src=16c6eed7-->
## Disjunctive Addition

<!--t src=e2ca832d-->
**Form:**
1. A.
2. Therefore A or B.

<!--t src=75120781-->
**Example:**
1. It is raining.
2. Therefore it is raining or it is snowing.

<!--t src=b80789dc-->
## Application in Critical Thinking

<!--t src=56f575a6-->
Understanding these patterns of argumentation is important for critical thinking for several reasons:

<!--t src=1b14c4a4-->
1. It enables the identification of valid argument structures in complex texts.

<!--t src=925b4845-->
2. It helps to recognize errors in the logical structure of arguments when they deviate from these patterns.

<!--t src=ca34d675-->
3. It provides tools for constructing one's own valid arguments.

<!--t src=661502af-->
4. It fosters a deeper understanding of the logical relationships between statements.
