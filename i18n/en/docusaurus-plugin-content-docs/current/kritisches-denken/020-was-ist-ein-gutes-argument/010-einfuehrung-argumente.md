In the previous chapters we got to know the foundations of critical thinking. Now we turn to a central question: **What is a good argument?**

<!--t src=eda0ef20-->
Before we distinguish good from bad arguments, the first parts clarify **what argumentation actually is**, what role it plays and **how an argument is structured**.

<!--t src=a0fbe6a6-->
## Why we argue

<!--t src=2fa817ef-->
We use arguments in everyday life and in discussions to **justify our views**, to support our positions and to convince other people. **Argumentations** are mostly dialogues in which we put forward reasons to support a claim.

<!--t src=d84a11e9-->
<!-- TODO: unvollständige Argumente -->

<!--t src=21ad2b03-->
But we do not always argue with the same goal. The following examples show typical conversational situations:

<!--t src=91a9f560-->
- Sometimes we argue **cooperatively**, in order to find the best solution together.

<!--t src=4b0d793b-->
:::info Example

_Example of a cooperative argumentation_

- _Hans_: Which phone provider are you with?
- _Lotte_: With Cheap-Tel
- _Hans_: And are you happy with the service? (_What are your reasons?_)
- _Lotte_: They have free texts and enough mobile data for a good price. (_Reason one and two, etc._)

:::

<!--t src=58b5b8f7-->
- Sometimes we want to **talk someone round**, by changing their opinion and showing them that there is a better way.

<!--t src=9bfe1875-->
:::info Example

_Example of a persuasive argumentation with reasons_

- _Hans_: Are you still with Dirty-Energy Ltd?
- _Lotte_: Sure, for years now, and I'll be staying too.
- _Hans_: But now there's EasyGreen. They're cheap, use only renewable energy and are a local cooperative. (_Reason one, reason two, ..._)
- _Lotte_: I don't care about the environment, but cheap and local sounds good, show me. (_Reasons are rejected or accepted_)

:::

<!--t src=faf33feb-->
- Often, however, we rather use emotions such as fear or hope to persuade or convince people.

<!--t src=397a086c-->
:::info Example

_Example of a persuasive argumentation with emotions_

- _Hans_: We have to spend more on defense.
- _Lotte_: Why?
- _Hans_: Otherwise Putin's army will soon be in Europe.

:::

<!--t src=98f657c0-->
- Sometimes we argue **against each other**, as often happens in politics.

<!--t src=e68123a4-->
:::info Example

_Example of an adversarial argumentation_

- _Hans_: The government wants to raise the inheritance tax. Isn't that terrible.
- _Lotte_: That you don't like it, with your three houses, I can understand, but I think it's right.
- _Hans_: Why on earth. It's always those who work hard who get punished. I want to leave something to my children and not give it away to the freeloaders.
- _Lotte_: Taxes are redistribution; without them there would be no social justice.
- _Hans_: Too much tax kills the tax base. If this keeps up, I'll move to the USA.

:::

<!--t src=70f7e514-->
As different as these situations are, they have one thing in common: we want to show that we are not mindlessly doing or deciding something, but are acting **for good reasons**. So we always have something that we believe and want to justify. Exactly how such an argument is structured, we will see in the next part.

## Overview of the chapter

<!--t src=dddfa8dc-->
### What is an argument?

<!--t src=a1221fda-->
We clarify the **structure of an argument** out of premises and conclusion, introduce the central terms (premise, conclusion, validity, soundness) and, with the **Toulmin model**, get to know a universal model that makes the internal construction of an argument visible.

<!--t src=726eea08-->
### Types of reasoning

<!--t src=febbc3d5-->
With **deduction**, **induction** and **abduction** you get to know the three basic forms of inference: what they presuppose, what they achieve and where their limits lie.

<!--t src=965a1db7-->
### Patterns of valid arguments

<!--t src=52dc7975-->
Here you get to know classical valid inference patterns, such as the **categorical syllogism**, the **reductio ad absurdum** and the **argument from analogy**, and practice recognizing them in everyday arguments.

<!--t src=e1d07e63-->
### Good and bad arguments

<!--t src=6ac9b3a6-->
The ability to **distinguish good from bad arguments** is a core competence of critical thinking. We clarify criteria such as **relevance**, **truth** of the premises and logical supporting force, so that you make better decisions in everyday life and protect yourself against manipulation.

<!--t src=adb85d67-->
### Rhetoric vs. argumentation

<!--t src=0640086d-->
We distinguish between **rhetorical persuasion** and **argumentative justification** and show how emotions, stylistic devices and framing work, and when they support or replace arguments.

<!--t src=8caa424e-->
### Formal and informal reasoning

<!--t src=d2ab57a3-->
We clarify the difference between the **formal inference rules** of logic and **informal arguing** in natural language, and when which checking strategy is appropriate.

<!--t src=0d288ab5-->
### Analyzing arguments and hidden assumptions

<!--t src=3878b491-->
Finally, we practice **breaking down** an argument, exposing its premises and conclusion and making **implicit assumptions** visible and examining them.
