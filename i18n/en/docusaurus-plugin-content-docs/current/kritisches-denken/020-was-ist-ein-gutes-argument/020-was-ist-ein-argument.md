<!-- # What is an argument? -->

<!--t src=a76dc362-->
An **argument** is more than a mere assertion. It consists of a claim **and** the reasons meant to support it. Whoever argues therefore does not just assert something, but also provides reasons for it.

<!--t src=5a805467-->
## Structure: premises and conclusion

<!--t src=e2f0960c-->
An argument has two building blocks:

<!--t src=345b6a9d-->
- **Premises**: the statements (reasons, evidence, assumptions) that we put forward to support our claim. The conversation partners should hold the premises to be true; but each reason can also be accepted or rejected.
- **Conclusion**: the claim that we try to support, that we hold to be true and that is in principle contestable.

<!--t src=794dd355-->
:::info Example

1. All humans are mortal. _(Premise 1)_
2. Socrates is a human. _(Premise 2)_
3. Therefore Socrates is mortal. _(Conclusion)_
   :::

<!--t src=3be02365-->
### Recognizing premises and conclusion

<!--t src=dec27466-->
In natural language the building blocks often give themselves away through **signal words**:

<!--t src=78cf3917-->
- A **conclusion** is often introduced by: _therefore_, _hence_, _thus_, _consequently_, _so_.
- A **premise** is often introduced by: _because_, _since_, _for_, _due to_, _after all_.

<!--t src=d38660f7-->
These words are useful signposts, but they are sometimes missing altogether. Then we have to work out for ourselves which statement is meant to be supported (conclusion) and which one supports it (premise).

<!--t src=fbd91caa-->
## Central terms

<!--t src=c171f78e-->
For the following chapters a shared vocabulary is useful:

<!--t src=87dc1fbd-->
- **Argument**: a combination of premises and a conclusion in which the premises are meant to support the conclusion.
- **Premise**: a supporting statement (a reason) of an argument.
- **Conclusion**: the statement that is meant to be supported by the premises.
- **Validity**: an argument is _valid_ if the conclusion follows from the premises &mdash; _if_ the premises are true, the conclusion _must_ be true too. Validity concerns only the **form**, not the truth content of the premises.
- **Soundness**: an argument is _sound_ if it is **valid** **and** its premises are actually **true**. Only sound arguments really give us good reasons.

<!--t src=90e5e176-->
:::note Key point
**Valid** = the conclusion follows from the premises. **Sound** = valid _and_ the premises are true.
:::

<!--t src=4cb3f919-->
## The Toulmin model: a universal way to describe arguments

<!--t src=159a8979-->
The British philosopher Stephen Toulmin developed a model that makes the internal construction of an argument visible. At its center is the **claim**, the contested statement we want to support. It rests on the **data (facts)**, the undisputed evidence. The bridge between data and claim is the **warrant**: a mostly unspoken rule that explains _why_ the data carry the claim. The warrant itself rests on the **backing**, that is, on experiential knowledge, laws or theories. The **qualifier** indicates how certain the claim is ("presumably", "as a rule"). Finally, the **rebuttal** names conditions under which the argument does not hold. In this way the model makes the hidden assumptions and weak points of an argumentation explicit.
