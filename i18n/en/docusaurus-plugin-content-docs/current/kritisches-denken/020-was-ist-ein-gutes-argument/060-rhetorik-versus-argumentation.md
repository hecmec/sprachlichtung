<!-- # Rhetoric versus argumentation -->

<!--t src=02433f6f-->
Rhetoric and argumentation are related but distinct disciplines. While both aim to convince others, they differ in their methods and goals.

<!--t src=f44d148f-->
## Argumentation

<!--t src=b8aa516c-->
**Argumentation** aims to convince through logical reasoning and evidence. It appeals primarily to the intellect and rationality of the audience.

<!--t src=f6d25064-->
Characteristics of argumentation:

<!--t src=dfe5c307-->
- Focus on logical structure and validity
- Emphasis on proof and evidence
- Goal: rational persuasion through reasons
- Consideration of counterarguments
- Transparency of premises and conclusions

<!--t src=bcaa80d7-->
## Rhetoric

<!--t src=9d1d3df4-->
**Rhetoric** is the art of persuasive communication, which encompasses not only logical arguments but also emotional appeals, style and presentation. It aims to influence the audience through various means.

<!--t src=fa5e5144-->
Characteristics of rhetoric:
- Use of language to persuade or move
- Consideration of the audience and the context
- Use of ethos (credibility), pathos (emotion) and logos (logic)
- Stylistic elements such as metaphors, analogies, repetition
- Goal: effective persuasion through various means

<!--t src=310171f2-->
## Differences and interplay

<!--t src=5c032933-->
The main difference between argumentation and rhetoric lies in their focus:
- Argumentation concentrates on the logical structure and the truth of the premises.
- Rhetoric concentrates on effective communication and persuasion.

<!--t src=b860efbd-->
In practice, argumentation and rhetoric are often interwoven. A good speaker or writer uses both solid arguments and rhetorical techniques to convince their audience.

<!--t src=b365bebc-->
**Example of pure argumentation:**
"All humans are mortal. Socrates is a human. Therefore Socrates is mortal."

<!--t src=0686baf0-->
**Example of rhetoric without substantive argumentation:**
"Do you really want to be one of those who deny this obvious truth? Any reasonable person can see that our position is the only correct one!"

<!--t src=44af6a99-->
**Example of the combination of argumentation and rhetoric:**
"Imagine a world in which children can grow up without fear of hunger. This is not a utopia, but an achievable goal. Studies show that targeted investments in agricultural education and infrastructure can increase food production in developing countries by up to 300%. By supporting our program, you can help make this vision a reality."

<!--t src=0c03b2a7-->
## Critical thinking and rhetoric

<!--t src=d275784f-->
For critical thinkers it is important to recognize rhetorical techniques and to distinguish between persuasive rhetoric and solid argumentation. Some questions that can help with this:

<!--t src=d9e9acd4-->
- What actual arguments are being presented?
- Are the arguments supported by evidence?
- Are emotional appeals used to bridge logical gaps?
- Is the speaker's credibility used as a substitute for evidence?
- Are counterarguments presented fairly and taken into account?
