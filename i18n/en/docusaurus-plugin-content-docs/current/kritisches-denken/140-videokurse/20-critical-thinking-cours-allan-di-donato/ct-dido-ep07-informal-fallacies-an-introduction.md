## Summary

This session begins a multi-part tour of **informal fallacies**. Normally a logic course teaches **formal (deductive) logic** first, but the order is reversed here because informal fallacies are **immediately applicable**: they are the errors you see constantly in articles, news broadcasts, and everyday conversation. The lecture opens with a list of flawed sample arguments (e.g. "she's blonde, so she must be stupid"; "same-sex marriage should be illegal because most people are against it") to revisit and diagnose once the categories are learned.

### Formal vs. informal logic

- **Formal logic** concerns the **form or structure** of an argument. Deductive arguments are judged **valid/invalid** and **sound/unsound**; with a correct form and true premises, the conclusion is **certain**.
- **Informal logic** concerns the **matter or content**. Inductive (and scientific) arguments are never valid or sound, only **strong or weak**, and their conclusions are at best **highly probable**, never certain.

### What a fallacy is

A **fallacy** is a mistake in reasoning that often looks correct until examined. Any fallacious argument is a **non sequitur** ("it does not follow"): the conclusion does not follow from its premises, so the argument must be rejected.

### Two kinds of fallacy

- **Formal fallacies** – errors in the **structure** of an argument, even when the premises are individually fine. Examples: the **four-term fallacy** in categorical syllogisms (a valid syllogism needs exactly three terms, usually failing for lack of a linking middle term), and **denying the antecedent** in a hypothetical syllogism ("if it's a circle, it's a geometric figure; it's *not* a circle", from which nothing follows).
- **Informal (material) fallacies** – errors in the **content**, arising from unclear meaning, irrelevance, or unsupported presumption.

### The three categories of informal fallacy

The course organizes informal fallacies into three families (a "fallacy family tree" descending from *fallacy → formal/informal → the three categories*):

1. **Fallacies of ambiguity** – the argument is vague or imprecise; terms are misused or grammar is faulty, making the content incoherent or inaccurate.
2. **Fallacies of relevance** – a premise has little or nothing to do with the conclusion. Three subdivisions:
   - **Attacking the source (ad hominem)** – attacking the arguer instead of the argument.
   - **Appeal to emotion** – swaying opinion through pity, anger, fear, or desire, all irrelevant to truth.
   - **Distraction** – shifting focus by changing the subject or proving something beside the point.
3. **Fallacies of presumption** – the argument rests on a hidden, unsupported, or questionable assumption. These split into **non-causal** (general unsupported assumptions) and **causal / inductive** ones that interfere with a reliable inductive process.

### Caveats

Textbooks organize and name these fallacies differently, and a single argument often commits **more than one** fallacy at once, so two careful analysts may legitimately spot different errors in the same example.
