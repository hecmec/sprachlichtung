# The Limits of Science

<!--t src=d99cab67-->
Despite its successes, science has inherent limits that need to be understood.

<!--t src=0ac62901-->
## Methodological Limits

<!--t src=d6549c7e-->
The scientific method itself has certain limitations:

<!--t src=45ed25bc-->
1. **Not all questions are scientifically investigable**: Questions about values, aesthetics or the meaning of life lie outside the scope of scientific investigation.

<!--t src=028a4f2b-->
   *Example*: The question "Is Beethoven a better composer than Mozart?" cannot be answered scientifically, since it involves aesthetic judgements.

<!--t src=6d9e4526-->
2. **Complexity and emergence**: Some phenomena are so complex or emergent that they are difficult to capture with reductionist methods.

<!--t src=5587b2cd-->
   *Example*: Consciousness, or complex social phenomena such as cultures.

<!--t src=1640cda0-->
3. **Unobservable phenomena**: Some phenomena are in principle not directly observable.

<!--t src=c7275c2d-->
   *Example*: Events before the Big Bang, or hypothetical parallel universes.

<!--t src=e6f08bee-->
4. **Ethical constraints**: Ethical considerations limit which experiments can be carried out.

<!--t src=68cab09f-->
   *Example*: Certain experiments on humans or animals are ethically unacceptable.

<!--t src=2b245b38-->
## Practical Limits

<!--t src=ebab623a-->
In addition to methodological limits, there are also practical ones:

<!--t src=6f79286e-->
1. **Resource constraints**: Scientific research requires time, money and expertise.

<!--t src=33b7c61d-->
   *Example*: Some experiments in particle physics require enormous resources, such as the Large Hadron Collider at CERN.

<!--t src=ddc4015d-->
2. **Technological constraints**: The current state of technology limits what can be measured or observed.

<!--t src=88d9b4a9-->
   *Example*: The observation of certain astronomical phenomena was not possible before the development of powerful telescopes.

<!--t src=93783940-->
3. **Cognitive constraints**: Human cognitive abilities have limits.

<!--t src=8dc1b353-->
   *Example*: The difficulty of intuitively understanding high-dimensional spaces or certain aspects of quantum mechanics.

<!--t src=9abdb608-->
## Science and Other Forms of Knowledge

<!--t src=aa14cefa-->
Science is not the only legitimate form of knowledge or cognition:

<!--t src=7b2b594f-->
1. **Personal experience**: Subjective experiences and insights that cannot be fully captured by scientific methods.

<!--t src=8425be35-->
   *Example*: The experience of love, grief or spiritual experiences.

<!--t src=48c91255-->
2. **Traditional knowledge**: Knowledge passed down over generations, which often contains practical wisdom.

<!--t src=83555095-->
   *Example*: Indigenous knowledge about local ecosystems or traditional healing methods.

<!--t src=007f5c9b-->
3. **Philosophical knowledge**: Logical analysis, conceptual clarification and normative reflection.

<!--t src=be1ef1de-->
   *Example*: Ethical theories or metaphysical questions.

<!--t src=f8421d5e-->
4. **Artistic knowledge**: Insights through artistic forms of expression.

<!--t src=fb9ba23d-->
   *Example*: Literary works that offer deep insights into human nature.

<!--t src=e5314f04-->
It is important to understand that these various forms of knowledge can be complementary and need not necessarily be in contradiction with science.

<!--t src=0a64dba6-->
## Science and Values

<!--t src=71cca8a9-->
An important limit of science concerns the relationship between facts and values:

<!--t src=621d1a95-->
1. **Is and ought**: Science can describe what is, but cannot directly derive what ought to be (Hume's law).

<!--t src=ddca3226-->
   *Example*: From the scientific finding that humans exhibit genetic differences, it does not follow how we ought to treat these people.

<!--t src=34beca43-->
2. **The value-ladenness of science**: Despite the ideal of value-freedom, scientific practices are themselves shaped by values.

<!--t src=ff6cfeeb-->
   *Example*: The selection of research questions, methods and interpretations is influenced by values.

<!--t src=fe2e16b8-->
3. **Science in its social context**: Scientific research takes place in a social, political and economic context that influences it.

<!--t src=2ea9e0f2-->
   *Example*: Research priorities are often shaped by funding sources and societal interests.
