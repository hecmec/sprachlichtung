<!-- ## The information flood and its pitfalls -->

<!--t src=0cbe513a-->
Not every false piece of information is a lie, and not every harmful piece of information is false. Anyone who wants to check sources should therefore distinguish the most important terms cleanly. The most influential classification comes from **Claire Wardle and Hossein Derakhshan** in their report _Information Disorder_ for the Council of Europe (2017). They distinguish along two questions: is the information **false or true**? And is there an **intent to harm** behind it?

<!--t src=7d0c92c4-->
| Term                          | False? | Intent to harm? | Example                                                                                |
| ----------------------------- | ------ | --------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Misinformation**            | yes    | no              | Someone shares a false rumour in good faith.                                           |
| **Disinformation**            | yes    | yes             | A made-up story is deliberately spread in order to do harm.                            |
| **Malinformation**            | no     | yes             | Real private data or true facts torn out of context are used as a weapon.              |

<!--t src=f9339921-->
Source: Wardle & Derakhshan, _Information Disorder_, Council of Europe 2017 ([PDF](https://rm.coe.int/information-disorder-report-version-august-2018/16808c9c77)).

<!--t src=70154f6d-->
The crucial point: the difference between **mis-** and **disinformation** is _not_ the truth content (both are false), but the **intent**. Whoever passes on a rumour unsuspectingly is engaging in misinformation; whoever invents it and spreads it deliberately, disinformation.

<!--t src=a6d46159-->
:::info Remember
**Misinformation = error. Disinformation = deception. Malinformation = truth used as a weapon.**
:::

<!--t src=c17ece5c-->
## Why „fake news“ is a bad term

<!--t src=17bc0c57-->
The popular term **„fake news“** sounds catchy, but it is of little use. Wardle and Derakhshan deliberately avoid it and call it „woefully inadequate“ for capturing the variety of „information pollution“.

<!--t src=4a7162c6-->
Two problems:

<!--t src=0cb69283-->
1. **It is vague.** Sometimes it means made-up stories, sometimes bad journalism, sometimes simply unwelcome reporting.
2. **It has become a weapon.** Politicians use „fake news“ as a battle cry to discredit reputable media wholesale.

<!--t src=13c362a6-->
Experts therefore prefer the precise terms **mis-, dis- and malinformation**. We do so in this chapter too.

<!--t src=eea127de-->
## Why our brain is vulnerable

<!--t src=e22e55b0-->
False information works because it exploits our psychology:

<!--t src=bf311a13-->
- **Confirmation bias:** we believe more readily what fits our opinion.
- **Repetition effect:** what we hear often we take to be truer („perceived truth“).
- **Emotion beats checking:** outrage, fear and enthusiasm make us share before we think.
- **Speed:** a lie is shared in seconds; its correction takes days.

<!--t src=1d79251f-->
These mechanisms are familiar from the chapter on **cognitive biases**. Source criticism is the deliberate countermeasure: it inserts a brief moment of checking between _stimulus_ and _response_.

<!--t src=97a794ed-->
:::warning Caution
The most dangerous false report is the one we _want_ to believe. Precisely with content that makes us immediately agree or feel outraged, checking pays off the most.
:::
