<!-- ## Fact-checking and verification tools -->

<!--t src=95dfeaa1-->
You don't have to check everything yourself; professionals have often already done so. And for the cases you take on yourself, there are good, free tools.

<!--t src=d96aa90f-->
## Reputable fact-checking services

<!--t src=298f43db-->
In the German-speaking world, fact-checkers include:

<!--t src=d81ae3c3-->
- **CORRECTIV.Faktencheck**: non-profit investigative centre ([correctiv.org/faktencheck](https://correctiv.org/faktencheck/))
- **ARD-Faktenfinder** (Tagesschau): public-service broadcasting ([tagesschau.de/faktenfinder](https://www.tagesschau.de/faktenfinder))
- **dpa-Faktencheck**: the German Press Agency
- **#Faktenfuchs** (Bayerischer Rundfunk)
- **Mimikama**: against internet abuse and hoaxes ([mimikama.org](https://www.mimikama.org))

<!--t src=09ae26a6-->
Internationally well known are **Snopes**, **PolitiFact** and **Full Fact**, as well as the **AFP Fact Check** of the news agency Agence France-Presse.

<!--t src=b9a990ff-->
:::tip My tip
Fact-checkers, too, are checked laterally. Reliability shows in transparency: are sources disclosed? Is there a visible correction practice? Is the funding identifiable?
:::

<!--t src=b0c85b88-->
### The IFCN seal of quality

<!--t src=d0d4b65a-->
A good sign to look for is membership of the **International Fact-Checking Network (IFCN)** at the Poynter Institute. Whoever signs its _Code of Principles_ (introduced in 2016) commits to five principles, compliance with which is verified by independent assessors ([ifcncodeofprinciples.poynter.org](https://ifcncodeofprinciples.poynter.org/the-commitments)):

<!--t src=8336258e-->
1. **Non-partisanship and fairness**: the same standard for everyone.
2. **Transparency of sources**: disclose evidence so that readers can verify it.
3. **Transparency of funding and organisation.**
4. **Transparency of methodology**: disclose how the checking is done.
5. **Open and honest corrections.**

<!--t src=efaa3344-->
Incidentally, these five principles are a good yardstick for _any_ source, not just for fact-checkers.

## A checking flowchart

<!--t src=97edd532-->
Anyone who wants to proceed in a structured way can follow the **EUfactcheck flowchart**, a teaching tool for journalism students. It divides the check into three steps ([eufactcheck.eu](https://eufactcheck.eu/about-us/eufactcheck-flowchart/)):

<!--t src=1614811d-->
1. **Analyse the claim**: what exactly is being claimed? Which part is even a verifiable fact?
2. **Analyse the author/source**: who is making the claim? With what credibility, expertise and possible motive?
3. **Fact check**: research the evidence and determine its accuracy.

<!--t src=763fad72-->
At its core this is the same logic as [SIFT](040-die-sift-methode.md), just more detailed.

## Tools for checking images and videos

<!--t src=9be1aae3-->
Images and videos are the most common carriers of disinformation, because they look so convincing. These tools help:

<!--t src=16c1330d-->
- **Reverse image search**: where else does an image appear, and how old is it?
  - [Google Images / Lens](https://images.google.com), [TinEye](https://tineye.com), [Yandex Images](https://yandex.com/images/) (often the most powerful).
- **InVID / WeVerify**: a free browser plugin that bundles many verification tools: extracting still frames from videos, reverse search, viewing metadata, magnifying parts of an image ([invid-project.eu](https://www.invid-project.eu/tools-and-services/invid-verification-plugin/)).
- **Geolocation**: with [Google Maps / Street View](https://maps.google.com) you can use buildings, signs or landscape to check whether an image was really taken at the claimed location.
- **EXIF data**: image files sometimes contain metadata (time taken, device, GPS). Readable with tools such as [Pic2Map](https://www.pic2map.com/). Caution: social networks usually strip this data on upload.

<!--t src=20ff6851-->
A practical German-language guide to geolocation is offered by CORRECTIV/GADMO: _[Geolocation: 5 Tipps, um den Aufnahmeort eines Bilds zu finden](https://correctiv.org/faktencheck/faktencheck-tipps/2023/06/08/geolocation-5-tipps-um-den-aufnahmeort-eines-bilds-zu-finden/)_.

## Practising checking skills

<!--t src=3bd1f32c-->
Anyone who wants to practise lateral reading with real examples will find free exercises and videos in English and French at the Canadian programme **Ctrl-F** (CIVIX) ([ctrl-f.ca](https://ctrl-f.ca/en/)). A study with over 2,300 students showed that the share of those who read laterally rose from 11% to 59% thanks to the programme: checking skills really are **trainable**.

<!--t src=222dd85f-->
:::tip In short
Check yourself _or_ use reputable fact-checkers, both are good. For images: **reverse search first.** It exposes most „out-of-context“ fakes in seconds.
:::
