## Methods and concepts

<!--t src=528623f5-->
- 🇬🇧 Mike Caulfield: [SIFT (The Four Moves)](https://hapgood.us/2019/06/19/sift-the-four-moves/). The original post in which Caulfield introduces the four steps of the SIFT method, the basis of the verification process covered in this chapter.
- 🇬🇧 Wineburg & McGrew: [Lateral Reading and the Nature of Expertise (Teachers College Record, 2019)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/016146811912101102). The academic study showing that professional fact-checkers judge faster and more reliably through lateral reading than experts who examine a page from the inside.
- 🇬🇧 UNESCO: [Media and Information Literacy](https://www.unesco.org/en/media-information-literacy). The official UNESCO page on the MIL concept, which brings together media and information literacy and provides the framework for this chapter.
- 🇬🇧 SBCC Library: [SIFT & PICK](https://libguides.sbcc.edu/siftpick). A library guide that combines the SIFT verification method with the PICK selection aid, showing how to both evaluate and appropriately choose sources.
- 🇬🇧 Meriam Library, CSU Chico: [CRAAP Test](https://library.csuchico.edu/help/source-or-information-good). The source of the well-known CRAAP test, a checklist for assessing the credibility of a source (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose).
- 🇬🇧 Saint Mary's University: [SIFT Method (PDF)](https://www.smu.ca/webfiles/SIFT_Method_final.pdf). A compact one-page summary of the SIFT method as a handout, handy for printing and taking along.

## Disinformation and research

<!--t src=fb8e688f-->
- 🇬🇧 Wardle & Derakhshan: [Information Disorder (Council of Europe, 2017, PDF)](https://rm.coe.int/information-disorder-report-version-august-2018/16808c9c77). The foundational report that coined the distinction between mis-, dis- and malinformation used in this chapter.
- 🇬🇧 Reuters Institute: [Echo chambers, filter bubbles and polarisation: a literature review](https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/echo-chambers-filter-bubbles-and-polarisation-literature-review). A research overview that summarizes the empirical state of knowledge on echo chambers and filter bubbles and shows why these claims are weaker than often assumed.
- 🇬🇧 Reuters Institute: [The truth behind filter bubbles: Bursting some myths](https://reutersinstitute.politics.ox.ac.uk/news/truth-behind-filter-bubbles-bursting-some-myths). An accessible write-up of this research that dispels widespread myths about filter bubbles.
- 🇬🇧 Eli Pariser: [Beware online filter bubbles (TED Talk)](https://www.ted.com/talks/eli_pariser_beware_online_filter_bubbles). The popular talk that made the term "filter bubble" widely known, included here as the historical starting point of the debate.
- 🇬🇧 Milli et al.: [Engagement, user satisfaction, and the amplification of divisive content (PNAS Nexus, 2025)](https://academic.oup.com/pnasnexus/article/4/3/pgaf062/8052060). A recent study showing how algorithms amplify divisive content by optimizing for engagement.
- 🇬🇧 EUvsDisinfo (EEAS): [euvsdisinfo.eu](https://euvsdisinfo.eu/). The database of the European External Action Service, which documents and exposes targeted (often state-run) disinformation campaigns.

## AI fakes

<!--t src=eac03e2f-->
- 🇬🇧 News Literacy Project: [RumorGuard / The 5 factors](https://newslit.org/news-and-research/dig-deeper-rumorguard-how-can-you-evaluate-the-credibility-of-an-online-claim-2/). A guide with five factors for quickly assessing the credibility of an online claim.
- 🇬🇧 NPR: [Fake viral images of an explosion at the Pentagon were probably created by AI](https://www.npr.org/2023/05/22/1177590231/fake-viral-images-of-an-explosion-at-the-pentagon-were-probably-created-by-ai). Reporting on an AI-generated fake image that serves as a concrete case study for deepfakes in this chapter.
- 🇬🇧 Al Jazeera: [Fake Pentagon explosion photo goes viral: How to spot an AI image](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2023/5/23/fake-pentagon-explosion-photo-goes-viral-how-to-spot-an-ai-image). A second write-up of the same case that also gives practical hints on how to recognize AI images.

## Fact-checking and tools

<!--t src=0f28a927-->
- 🇬🇧 IFCN: [Code of Principles / The Commitments](https://ifcncodeofprinciples.poynter.org/the-commitments). The code of conduct of the International Fact-Checking Network, by which you can recognize reputable, independent fact-checkers.
- 🇩🇪 CORRECTIV: [Faktencheck](https://correctiv.org/faktencheck/). The fact-checking section of a well-known German investigative center, a central point of reference for verified claims in the German-speaking world.
- 🇩🇪 ARD: [Faktenfinder (Tagesschau)](https://www.tagesschau.de/faktenfinder). The fact-checking service of Tagesschau, which verifies false reports currently in circulation.
- 🇬🇧 EUfactcheck: [Flowchart](https://eufactcheck.eu/about-us/eufactcheck-flowchart/). A flowchart that visualizes the fact-checking process step by step.
- 🇬🇧 CIVIX: [Ctrl-F: Find the Facts](https://ctrl-f.ca/en/). A free learning program that trains lateral reading and quick online verification.
- 🇬🇧 InVID/WeVerify: [Verification Plugin](https://www.invid-project.eu/tools-and-services/invid-verification-plugin/). A free browser tool for verifying images and videos, for example via reverse image search and metadata analysis.
- 🇩🇪 CORRECTIV/GADMO: [Geolocation: 5 Tipps, um den Aufnahmeort eines Bilds zu finden](https://correctiv.org/faktencheck/faktencheck-tipps/2023/06/08/geolocation-5-tipps-um-den-aufnahmeort-eines-bilds-zu-finden/). A practical guide on how to determine the actual location where a photo was taken from features in the image.

## Wikipedia

<!--t src=bdd2b0cb-->
- 🇩🇪 [Wikipedia:Belege](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Belege). The guideline requiring Wikipedia to back up statements with reliable sources; it explains why the references lead you to the primary sources.
- 🇩🇪 [Wikipedia:Gesichtete Versionen](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Gesichtete_Versionen). The explanation of the flagged-revisions system, which describes how Wikipedia catches obvious vandalism before it is displayed.
- 🇬🇧 [Reliability of Wikipedia (with Nature study 2005)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reliability_of_Wikipedia). An overview article on the reliability of Wikipedia that, drawing on the well-known Nature study, shows that Wikipedia is a good starting point but not an end point.

## Further learning materials

<!--t src=803b84ac-->
- 🇬🇧 Mike Caulfield: [Web Literacy for Student Fact-Checkers](https://pressbooks.pub/webliteracy/). This book teaches how to use the internet as a fact-checking machine and is written by a well-known expert in <abbr title="Media and Information Literacy">MIL</abbr>. It is also where we find the SIFT method.
- 🇬🇧 [Introduction to College Research: Disinformation & Fact-Checking](https://pressbooks.pub/introtocollegeresearch/part/introduction/). A very good online book on media literacy and source criticism (_information literacy skills_) with an open license (OER, CC-BY).
- 🇩🇪 [European Journalism Observatory](https://de.ejo-online.eu/). A journalism trade portal that presents media research and industry trends in an accessible way and offers a look behind the scenes of news coverage.
